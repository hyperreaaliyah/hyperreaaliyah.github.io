About

HYPERREAALIYAH was founded in 2014 through a process that involved the writings of Jean Baudrillard, one (1) BTC worth of acid in that day’s exchange rate, and a handful of people. Since the beginning, HYPERREAALIYAH has existed as a simulation of an artist, born again for each new project through the collaborators of said project. Sometimes for clarity HYPERREAALIYAH is referred to as a collective, although it has no formal membership but becomes realised through projects for which a new team is always formed. Both professional artists and non-artists have participated in the production of works of HYPERREAALIYAH. The name HYPERREAALIYAH consists of two parts; (1) hyperreal which translates to “more real than real”. This is a category often experienced in a mediated world where the representation of a thing becomes more real than the thing itself. For example, a news story about a hurricane on the other side of the globe is more real to its reader than the hurricane itself; and (2) aaliyah which is a judaic concept meaning a return to the holy land. Thus, HYPERREAALIYAH is a more real than real return to the holy lands of, in this case, meaning. The premise for HYPERREAALIYAH, the reason for its existence is the argument that as hyperreality has become an everyday phenomenon, we are all in diaspora from meaning. HYPERREAALIYAH is always looking for a way to return to meaning, but never quite seems to get there. Throughout the past years, HYPERREAALIYAH has produced multiple different projects ranging from video works to performances and artivistic interventions in public, semi-public and private spaces both online and offline. HYPERREAALIYAH's works have been screened, performed or featured in Amos Rex, Institute of Contemporary Art London, Kaiku, Oksasenkatu 11, POLARIZED! festival, Pixelache, Socialinis Centras Emma, Visio festival and XI20, among others. HYPERREAALIYAH has researched and produced works about memes and memetics in particular and works critiquing neoliberal ideology and fascistic societal movements in general.

7/12/2016

Yesterday we (sort of) celebrated the independence day of Finland. Many demonstrations were held in Helsinki. The most problematic of them was a march organized by the Nordic Resistance Movement, a Swedish political party and a Neo-Nazi organization. In addition to Finnish members of the group, there were also Swedish Nazis who came to participate on the march. We decided to disturb their militaristic march over the Pitkäsilta bridge by walking next to them with a speaker blasting 'Den glider in', the victory song which the Swedes made in 1995 in anticipation of winning Finland in the finals of the Ice Hockey World Championship. Contrary to the glib expectations of the Swedes, 1995 became the first time Finland won the Championship and thus we took their song to celebrate our victory. MM95, as it is referred to in Finland, is an emotional soft spot of nationalistic pride for many Finns. By playing the song and singing it loud to the marching Nazis, we hoped to stir contradictory emotions in the Finns and Swedes who participated while implying that we have already won Sweden in the Blackberry Winter War of 1995. We have some audiovisual documentation of this action which might be released later.
